Source: IRC § 263A — Uniform Capitalization Rules; Treas. Reg. § 1.263A-1

IRC § 263A — Capitalization Rules for Construction

OVERVIEW
Section 263A requires taxpayers to capitalize direct and indirect costs allocable to property produced by the taxpayer. For tax-exempt organizations, this is primarily relevant for GAAP compliance rather than tax computation.

COSTS REQUIRED TO BE CAPITALIZED:
Direct costs:
- Direct materials (building materials, fixtures)
- Direct labor (construction wages, contractor payments)

Indirect costs that must be capitalized:
- Indirect labor (supervision, project management)
- Utilities during construction
- Insurance during construction period
- Taxes during construction (property tax on land)
- Interest (per ASC 835-20)
- Professional fees (architect, engineer, legal — related to construction)
- Permits and licenses

Costs NOT capitalized (period costs):
- General and administrative overhead not related to construction
- Marketing and selling costs
- Research and development
- Fundraising costs
- Organizational costs

WHEN TO STOP CAPITALIZING
Costs are capitalized until the property is placed in service (PIS). After PIS:
- Operating costs are expensed as incurred
- Debt service interest is expensed
- Repairs and maintenance are expensed (unless they meet capitalization threshold)

GAAP vs TAX DIFFERENCES
GAAP (ASC 835-20): interest capitalization based on avoidable interest concept
Tax (§ 263A): interest capitalization based on accumulated production expenditures
For tax-exempt organizations, GAAP treatment is the relevant standard.

RI-SPECIFIC CONTEXT
RI's CIP tracks all capitalizable costs during construction using two cost categories:
- HARD_COST: direct construction costs (materials, labor, subcontractors)
- SOFT_COST: indirect costs (architecture, engineering, legal, permits, insurance, interest)
At PIS date, CIP is converted to fixed asset components and depreciation begins.
